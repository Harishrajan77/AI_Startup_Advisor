# Lean Startup and Customer Discovery Frameworks

This document guides founders on validating their startup ideas using Lean Startup principles and structured Customer Discovery.

## 1. The Core Lean Startup Methodology
The Lean Startup framework, pioneered by Eric Ries, focuses on eliminating waste and speeding up the product development cycle. Instead of writing a 50-page business plan and building a product in isolation for months, founders should use the **Build-Measure-Learn** feedback loop.

```
       [ BUILD ] (Minimal Viable Product)
         /   \
        /     \
    [LEARN] <--- [MEASURE] (Customer Data)
```

- **Build**: Develop the smallest possible version of the product that allows you to start learning (the Minimum Viable Product, or MVP).
- **Measure**: Collect qualitative and quantitative feedback from actual users.
- **Learn**: Decide whether to **pivot** (change a major aspect of the business model) or **persevere** (keep executing the current path).

## 2. Customer Discovery and "The Mom Test"
Customer discovery, defined by Steve Blank, is the process of identifying who your customers are and whether your product solves a real problem for them.
Rob Fitzpatrick's *The Mom Test* outlines how to talk to customers and get honest feedback, even when they want to be polite:

1. **Talk about their life, not your idea**: Ask how they currently handle a specific problem. Do not say: "I have an idea for an AI app, would you buy it?" Instead, ask: "How do you currently track your workout progress?"
2. **Ask about specifics in the past, not generics about the future**: Do not ask: "Would you pay $10/month for this?" Instead, ask: "When was the last time you paid for a fitness subscription? How much did it cost? Why did you buy it?"
3. **Talk less and listen more**: Let the customer explain their pain points. If they mention a workaround they created, you have found a high-value problem.

## 3. The Value Proposition Canvas
To design a successful product, founders must align their value proposition with customer profiles:

- **Customer Profile**:
  - **Jobs-to-be-Done**: What tasks is the customer trying to perform or solve in their work or life?
  - **Pains**: What makes these tasks difficult, expensive, or annoying?
  - **Gains**: What positive outcomes or benefits does the customer expect or desire?
  - 
- **Value Map**:
  - **Products & Services**: What list of products/services do you offer to help them do those jobs?
  - **Pain Relievers**: How do your products/services alleviate specific customer pains?
  - **Gain Creators**: How do your products/services produce the outcomes/benefits the customer wants?
