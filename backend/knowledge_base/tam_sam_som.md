# Market Sizing: TAM, SAM, and SOM Framework

To validate a startup idea, founders must determine if the market size justifies the investment. This is done by segmenting the market into TAM, SAM, and SOM.

```
+-------------------------------------------------------+
| TAM: Total Addressable Market                         |
|   +-----------------------------------------------+   |
|   | SAM: Serviceable Addressable Market           |   |
|   |   +---------------------------------------+   |   |
|   |   | SOM: Serviceable Obtainable Market    |   |   |
|   |   +---------------------------------------+   |   |
|   +-----------------------------------------------+   |
+-------------------------------------------------------+
```

## 1. Defining the Segments

### TAM (Total Addressable Market)
TAM is the total market demand for a product or service. It represents the maximum annual revenue your business could generate if you achieved 100% market share in your industry globally.
*Formula*: `TAM = Total Number of Target Customers (Globally) * Annual Contract Value (ACV) or Revenue per User (ARPU)`

### SAM (Serviceable Addressable Market)
SAM is the portion of the TAM that is within your geographic reach, technology capabilities, and business model. It is the subset of the market that actually buys the type of product you sell.
*Formula*: `SAM = Target Customers in Reached Geographies/Segments * ARPU`

### SOM (Serviceable Obtainable Market)
SOM is the portion of your SAM that you can realistically capture within your first 3 to 5 years, accounting for competition, marketing budgets, and execution capacity.
*Formula*: `SOM = Capturable Market Share (%) * SAM`

## 2. Calculation Approaches

### Top-Down Sizing
Uses secondary research, industry reports (e.g., Gartner, Forrester), and macroeconomic data to estimate market sizes. 
*Example*: "The global fitness market is $100 Billion (TAM). Digital fitness is 15% ($15B, SAM). We expect to capture 1% ($150M, SOM)."
*Caution*: Top-down sizing is often less reliable because it assumes a generic market share percentage without operational validation.

### Bottom-Up Sizing (Recommended)
Calculates market size using primary data, local demographics, and actual pricing models.
*Example*: An AI-powered fitness app for college students.
- **TAM**: There are 200 million college students globally. If we price the app at $5/month ($60/year), TAM is:
  `200M students * $60/year = $12 Billion`
- **SAM**: Our app initially supports only English and launches in the United States, which has 20 million college students. SAM is:
  `20M students * $60/year = $1.2 Billion`
- **SOM**: In our first 3 years, we target students in 50 universities, totaling 1 million students. We aim for 5% adoption in these schools (50,000 active users). SOM is:
  `50,000 users * $60/year = $3 Million`

## 3. Evaluating Market Sizing Results
- **Too Small (SOM < $1M / TAM < $10M)**: May not be venture-backable. Best suited as a lifestyle business or side project unless pricing can be raised significantly.
- **Healthy (SOM $2M - $10M / TAM $100M - $1B)**: A great sweet spot for early-stage venture backing or high-margin bootstrapping.
- **Unrealistically Large**: If a bottom-up SOM is > $50M in year 3 for a raw startup, check assumptions on conversion rates, pricing tier distributions, and competition.
